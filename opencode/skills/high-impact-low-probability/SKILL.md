# High-Impact/Low-Probability Analysis

Use this framework when analysts or decision-makers are convinced an event is unlikely but have not given much thought to the consequences if it occurs. This technique sensitizes analysts to the potential impact of seemingly low-probability events that would have major repercussions.

From *A Tradecraft Primer: Structured Analytic Techniques for Intelligence Analysis* (CIA, 2009). Examples where this would have helped: fall of the Shah (1979), collapse of the Soviet Union (1991), reunification of Germany (1990), Pearl Harbor (1941), 9/11 attacks (2001). All were considered low probability at the time; analysts would have benefited from considering the consequences and how they might plausibly come about.

## Core Principle

**Postulate the opposite of the consensus.** If there is a strongly held view that an event is unlikely, then postulating precisely the opposite should not be difficult. The goal is not to predict the event but to: (1) map hidden relationships between key factors, (2) alert analysts to oversights in the mainstream line, and (3) develop signposts for early warning.

## Stages

### 1. DEFINE THE HIGH-IMPACT OUTCOME CLEARLY
**IMPORTANT: You MUST use `ai_venice_web_search` to research historical cases of similar low-probability, high-impact events. Do NOT rely on training data.**

- State the specific outcome clearly and precisely — what exactly would happen?
- Define the scope: geographic, temporal, systemic boundaries
- Establish why this outcome would have major consequences — who would be affected, how severely, for how long?
- This step justifies examining what most analysts believe is a very unlikely development
- Search for historical analogies: when has a similar unlikely event occurred? What were the warning signs?

### 2. DEVISE PLAUSIBLE PATHWAYS

Develop one or more plausible explanations or "pathways" to the low-probability outcome:
- Each pathway should be as precise as possible — specific chain of events, not vague speculation
- Pathways should be grounded in logic and evidence, not fantasy
- Multiple pathways are expected — different routes to the same outcome
- For each pathway, identify:
  - The starting conditions (what must already be true)
  - The triggering event(s) that start the chain
  - The intermediate steps that propagate the chain
  - The final outcome

### 3. IDENTIFY TRIGGERS AND MOMENTUM SHIFTS

Insert possible triggers or changes in momentum that could accelerate the pathway:
- Natural disasters, sudden health problems of key leaders
- Economic or political shocks (financial crises, coups, assassinations)
- Technological disruptions, military accidents, terrorist attacks
- External interventions or withdrawals
- Brainstorm with analysts having broad experience to identify plausible but unpredictable triggers

### 4. DEVELOP SIGNPOSTS AND INDICATORS

For each pathway, identify a set of indicators or "observables" that would help anticipate that events are beginning to play out this way:
- **Early warning indicators:** Signs that the preconditions are developing
- **Trigger indicators:** Signs that a triggering event is becoming more likely
- **Momentum indicators:** Signs that the chain reaction is accelerating
- **Outcome indicators:** Signs that the final outcome is becoming more likely
- Indicators should be specific, observable, and measurable — not vague "increased tensions"
- Distinguish between indicators that the outcome is emerging vs. indicators that it is NOT emerging

### 5. IDENTIFY DEFLECTING AND ENCOURAGING FACTORS

- **Deflecting factors:** What could prevent or mitigate the bad outcome? (contingency planning, hedging strategies)
- **Encouraging factors:** What could accelerate or worsen the outcome? (feedback loops, cascading failures)
- This transforms the analysis from passive warning to actionable preparation

### 6. PERIODICALLY REVIEW AND UPDATE

- Review the indicators list periodically (monthly, quarterly, or as events warrant)
- Update the pathways as new information becomes available
- Re-evaluate the probability assessment as conditions change
- The goal is to counter the prevailing mind-set that the development is "highly unlikely" — periodic review forces reconsideration

## Value Added

- **Uncovers hidden relationships:** Mapping the course of an unlikely yet plausible event reveals connections between key factors and assumptions that are not visible in the mainstream analysis
- **Alerts to oversights:** The mainstream analytic line may have blind spots that become visible only when the opposite is systematically examined
- **Develops early warning:** Signposts provide concrete, observable criteria for detecting shifts before they become obvious
- **Counters mind-set:** Periodic review of indicators helps counter the natural tendency to dismiss unlikely events and stop monitoring them
- **Supports hedging:** Understanding pathways and triggers allows decision-makers to develop contingency plans and hedging strategies

## Relationship to Other Techniques

- **Premortem:** Assumes the event HAS happened and works backward to find causes. High-Impact/Low-Probability assumes the event MIGHT happen and works forward to find pathways and signposts.
- **"What If?" Analysis:** Similar but focuses more on explaining HOW the event might come about rather than on consequences and signposts. High-Impact/Low-Probability dwells more on the consequences and early warning.
- **Indicators/Signposts:** High-Impact/Low-Probability generates a specific set of indicators for a specific unlikely outcome; general Indicators/Signposts technique tracks multiple developments over time.
- **Red Team:** Red Team actively tries to cause the outcome; High-Impact/Low-Probability maps how it might happen naturally.

## Scope and Limitations

**Works on:** Strategic planning, risk assessment, early warning systems, contingency planning, investment decisions, policy planning, any domain where unlikely events could have catastrophic or transformative consequences.

**Does NOT work on:** Routine operational decisions, problems with only one plausible outcome, situations where the probability can be reliably estimated (use statistical methods instead), personal life decisions (use WRAP or decision-record instead).

**Limitations:**
- Pathways are speculative by nature — they are plausible, not probable
- Analysts may be reluctant to spend time on "unlikely" events
- Signposts may produce false positives (indicators appear but event does not occur)
- Requires periodic review to be effective — one-time analysis is insufficient
- Can be gamed by those who want to argue for a specific outcome
