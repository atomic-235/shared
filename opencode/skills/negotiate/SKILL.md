# Negotiate Framework (Multi-Stakeholder Conflict Resolution)

Use this framework when two or more parties have partially opposed interests and need to reach a negotiated agreement (or determine that no agreement is preferable to a bad one). Combines principled negotiation (Fisher-Ury), conflict mode selection (Thomas-Kilmann), tactical empathy (Voss), escalation-stage diagnosis (Glasl), and coalition strategy for the multi-party case.

Originated in the Harvard Negotiation Project (1979), codified by Roger Fisher, William Ury, and Bruce Patton in *Getting to Yes* (1981). Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (1974) adds situational style selection. Chris Voss's *Never Split the Difference* (2016) layers FBI hostage negotiation tactics. Friedrich Glasl's escalation ladder provides pre-negotiation diagnosis.

## Core Concepts

**Position vs Interest** — Position is what a party says they want. Interest is why they want it. Fisher-Ury's central insight: negotiate over interests, not positions. Positional bargaining locks parties into a fixed pie; interest-based negotiation expands it.

**BATNA (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement)** — Your best outcome if negotiation fails and you walk away. BATNA is the source of negotiation power — never accept a deal worse than BATNA, never reject one better. Develops the reservation point (walk-away threshold).

**Reservation Point** — Walk-away threshold, derived from BATNA. For seller = lowest acceptable price; for buyer = highest acceptable price. Estimate THEIR reservation point too.

**ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)** — Overlap between parties' reservation points. No overlap → no deal possible without changes (add issues, switch to integrative frame, improve BATNA). ZOPA is dynamic — early offers and concessions probe it.

**Distributive vs Integrative** — Distributive = win-lose, fixed pie, single issue, one-off, low trust. Integrative = win-win, expand the pie, multi-issue, ongoing relationship, trust. Real negotiations usually blend both: create value integratively then divide distributively.

**Escalation Level (Glasl ladder)** — Conflict escalation staged 1-9 in 3 tiers. Tier 1 (1-3: win-lose, debate, polarization): negotiation viable. Tier 2 (4-6: threats, counter-tactics): negotiation difficult, needs de-escalation first. Tier 3 (7-9: limited destructive blows, fragmentation, threat to exist): negotiation usually not viable until third-party mediation.

## Stages

### 1. PREPARE AND DIAGNOSE
**IMPORTANT: You MUST use the `ai_venice_web_search` tool to research the counterpart, current market context, and comparable agreements. Do NOT rely on training data.**

**1a. Scope the negotiation**
- Who are the parties? (Bilateral? Multi-party? Representatives vs principals?)
- What are the issues on the table? (One issue or many?)
- Is this one-off or ongoing relationship?
- What is the time horizon and deadline?
- What authority does each party have to commit?

**1b. Identify positions AND interests (per party)**
- For each party (including yourself): write their stated positions AND the underlying interests behind them.
- For each position, ask "why do they want that?" three times to peel down to interests.
- Interests include: substantive (money, terms), procedural (how decisions get made), psychological (respect, autonomy, recognition, fear, face-saving).

**1c. Develop your BATNA**
1. List ALL conceivable actions you could take if no agreement is reached
2. Evaluate each alternative by your interests
3. Pick the best — that's your BATNA
4. Strengthen your BATNA where possible before talks start
5. Set your reservation point (walk-away threshold) from BATNA

**1d. Estimate counterpart BATNA + reservation point**
- Use market data, prior deals, comparable agreements — search for these
- Identify leverage points where their BATNA is weak
- Critical: do not assume their stated position IS their reservation point

**1e. Estimate ZOPA**
- If your reservation point and their estimated reservation point overlap: ZOPA exists
- If not: either work to expand issues / change frame / add variables, or prepare to walk

**1f. Determine distributive vs integrative mode**
- Multi-issue and ongoing relationship → lean integrative
- Single issue, one-off, low trust → distributive
- Most negotiations are mixed — start integrative to create value, then distributive to divide it

**1g. Diagnose conflict escalation level (Glasl)**
- Tier 1 (win-lose, debate, polarization): proceed with negotiation
- Tier 2 (threats, public stances, ultimatums, "images of enemy"): de-escalation FIRST — defer substantive talks. Apply: cross-mechanism communication channels, confidence-building measures, neutral facilitation.
- Tier 3 (limited destructive blows, fragmentation, target = annihilation): defer substantive negotiation. Refer to mediation / third-party intervention. This skill is out of scope at Tier 3.

**1h. Set your MDO, Goal, LAA**
- MDO (Most Desired Outcome): best realistic outcome
- Goal: most likely good outcome
- LAA (Least Acceptable Agreement): equal to or just above reservation point
- For multi-party: estimate coalition alignments, their BATNAs (use a payoff matrix of parties × issues). Identify winning vs blocking coalitions.

### 2. OPEN AND EMPATHIZE
Tactical empathy — Voss techniques. Goal: build trust, gather information, defer your position.

**2a. Accusation Audit**
- Before substantive talk, list every negative thing they could possibly say about you / your proposal
- Preempt each: "You're probably going to think I'm being greedy / unfair / unreasonable..."
- Defuses by acknowledging before they can weaponize

**2b. Mirrors** (repeat last 1-3 words of their statement, upward inflection)
- Triggers elaboration without leading
- Best at start — gather information, don't give any

**2c. Labels** ("It seems like...", "It sounds like...", "It looks like...")
- Name their emotion. Don't ask "Are you feeling X?" — assert tentatively
- Right label: buy-in and emotional release. Wrong label: they correct you, revealing more

**2d. Calibrated Questions** ("How" / "What" — never "Why" or "Yes/No")
- "How am I supposed to do that?" (shifts problem-solving to them)
- "What about this works for you?"
- "What is the biggest challenge you face here?"
- Forces them to solve your problem; education without confrontation

**2e. Seek "That's right"**
- The breakthrough moment. Synthesize their worldview back to them. Achieve "that's right" — they feel heard, drop defenses.
- "Yes" is often a fake agreement signaling escape; "No" is the start of real negotiation.

### 3. EXPLORE INTERESTS — Fisher-Ury 4 Principles

**3a. Separate the people from the problem**
- The other party is not the adversary — the SITUATION is. Stand shoulder-to-shoulder facing the mutual problem.
- Address perception, emotion, communication separately from substantive issues.
- Don't react to attacks on you as attacks — reframe as attacks on the problem.

**3b. Focus on interests, not positions**
- For each stated position on either side, identify the underlying interest
- Surface shared interests (often more than either party assumes)
- Surface differing interests — these are where trades happen (a difference can create value)

**3c. Invent options for mutual gain**
- Brainstorm WITHOUT judging. Don't decide while generating.
- Use "What if..." / "Would it help if..." probes
- Look for trades: low cost to you + high value to them (or vice versa)
- Add new issues to the table — expanding the pie often creates overlap where none existed
- Use the One-Text Procedure: one party drafts a single agreement text, all parties propose edits iteratively. Avoids yes/no ping-pong, builds toward common ground.

**3d. Insist on objective criteria**
- Fair standards: market rate, precedent, professional standards, law, equal treatment, reciprocal treatment
- "Let's see what's fair here" rather than "I want X"
- Frame as: "I'll accept any outcome that meets [fair criterion]" — principled, not positional

### 4. BARGAIN AND DECIDE — ZOPA Navigation

**4a. Check conflict style (Thomas-Kilmann) for this phase**

| Style | When to use |
|---|---|
| **Competing** | After value creation, dividing scarce resources. When counterpart violates good-faith norms. Stakes high + relationship unimportant. |
| **Collaborating** | Default for value creation in stage 3. Stakes high + relationship long-term. |
| **Compromising** | Time pressure. Issue moderate-importance. Quick resolutions needed. |
| **Avoiding** | Emotional escalation deferral ("Let's take a recess"). Trivial-issue deflection. |
| **Accommodating** | Their interest vastly exceeds yours at minimal cost. Building goodwill for future. |

No single style is best across situations. The skill is consciously switching modes mid-negotiation.

**4b. Tactical moves (if distributive component exists)**
- Ackerman Bargaining (Voss): target price → open at 65% → 85% → 95% → 100% with decreasing increment. Use calibrated questions between offers.
- Never split the difference reflexively — splitting only works if both reservation points genuinely overlap in the middle.
- Use calibrated questions: "How am I supposed to do that?" when their offer is outside your reservation point.
- "I'm sorry but I'm afraid I just can't do that" — express inability, triggers counterparty empathy.

**4c. Monitor BATNA throughout**
- Re-evaluate BATNA as information surfaces
- If new info improves BATNA → walk-away threshold rises
- If counterparty's BATNA improves (new competing offer appears) → their reservation hardens; tighten urgency or pivot style
- Always be ready to walk. Walking to BATNA is not failure — accepting a worse-than-BATNA deal IS failure.

**4d. Expand or escape narrow ZOPA**
- Add new issues (bundle)
- Reframe issue from fixed pie to integrated trade
- Use objective criteria to settle terms within ZOPA
- If no ZOPA after good-faith exploration: walk to BATNA. Document why for review.

### 5. CLOSE AND REVIEW

**5a. Commit to agreement that exceeds BATNA**
- Final terms must beat your BATNA on all weighted dimensions
- Use objective criteria for final numbers, not "because I said so"
- Protect face — your own AND theirs. Face-saving bridges: "Here is what has already changed since we started," "We both agreed to fair norms X, Y."

**5b. Document the agreement**
- One-text: single written agreement, explicitly ratified
- Avoid post-agreement ambiguity (renegotiation, scope creep)
- Capture intent, not just numbers

**5c. Preserve the relationship**
- Ongoing relationship: schedule a post-mortem with the counterpart too
- Plan implementation: who does what by when. Vague implementation = future conflict.
- Reciprocity deposits: act consistently with the deal's spirit, not just letter.

**5d. Post-mortem (your side)**
- What worked? What didn't?
- Did you set MDO / Reservation / BATNA correctly? Did you estimate THEIR reservation well?
- Which TK mode did you actually use? Was it the right one?
- Did you accept a deal slightly worse than BATNA?
- Did you misread emotional cues?
- Update negotiation playbook for next time.

**5e. Monitor for breach / drift**
- Set tripwires: what would signal renegotiation needed?
- Compare delivered to promised at agreed intervals
- If repeated game: early-cooperation signals set up trust for later rounds

## Multi-Party Addendum

When >2 parties, additional complexity layers on top:

**Coalitions:**
- Pre-negotiation stage is critical: coalitions form BEFORE substantive bargaining
- Identify winning coalitions (sufficient votes/approval for a deal) vs blocking coalitions (sufficient to prevent a deal)
- Coalitions are inherently unstable: members' interests can shift during the process
- Track payoff matrix: party × issue × position × interest. Update as offers reshape BATNAs.

**Procedural moves:**
- Miniaturization (Ambassador Tommy Koh, Law of the Sea): include all parties in education/initial phase, then move substantive negotiation to a manageable subgroup
- One-Text Procedure becomes dominant tool — single draft agreement with iterative edits

**Neutrality perception is critical:**
- Don't be seen as forming coalitions that exclude neutrals
- A neutral chairperson or facilitator may be required
- Cross-communication across party lines keeps perceptions of fairness intact

**Game-theoretic check:**
- Ask "given others' strategies, can any coalition unilaterally improve?" If yes, your coalition is unstable
- Sequence moves to make defection less attractive
- BATNA in multi-party is more complex — coalition membership IS often the BATNA enhancer

## Emotional Intelligence Layer (Cross-Cutting)

Apply throughout, not just in stage 2:

- **Self-awareness:** Name your own emotional triggers before talks. Pause when triggered.
- **Emotional regulation:** When reactive, breathe, slow tempo, don't draft a response in emotional heat.
- **Counterpart regulation:** Notice their upset; pause substantive talk and inquire about feelings first. Shift from oppositional ("me vs you") to shoulder-to-shoulder ("us vs the problem").
- **Accusation audit:** Also useful mid-negotiation if emotional climate shifts.
- **Mindfulness post-mortem:** Stage 5d includes emotional review — what triggered you? What did you miss?

## How This Differs From Other Skills

**vs. systems-thinking:** Systems-thinking maps feedback loops as OBSERVER. Negotiate is the STAKEHOLDER-ENGAGEMENT process — prescribes communicative moves to bring parties from opposed positions to agreement. Use systems-thinking first to map incentives, then negotiate to act.

**vs. link-analysis:** Link-analysis draws the network for analysis (static). Negotiate uses the network to decide who to engage, what coalitions to form. Use link-analysis to map, negotiate to act.

**vs. wrap:** WRAP is single-party anti-bias decision-making. No counterpart. Negotiate is two+ parties with opposed interests requiring mutual adjustment. WRAP can be used WITHIN negotiate's stage 1 to widen options.

**vs. red-team:** Red-team adversarially attacks your own plan. No external counterpart. Negotiate has an external, independent counterpart who cannot be replaced by self-critique.

**vs. ACH:** ACH evaluates competing explanations of evidence — pure analysis, no negotiation counterpart. Negotiate is parties trading along a ZOPA.

**vs. triz:** TRIZ resolves TECHNICAL contradictions via inventive principles. Negotiate resolves STAKEHOLDER INTEREST contradictions.

**vs. ooda-loop:** OODA is fast iterative sense-act cycles. Negotiate uses OODA-like iteration within each stage. They nest, not compete.

## Examples by Domain

**Business — Salary negotiation (mixed distributive+integrative):**
- Prepare: research comparable salaries. Your interests: pay, role scope, growth. Their interests: budget, retention, market fit. BATNA = competing offer in hand. Reservation: industry 25th percentile. ZOPA: 25th-75th percentile.
- Open: accusation audit ("You might think I'm overreaching..."); calibrated question ("What does great performance in this role look like in year 1?") to surface what they value.
- Explore: bundle base salary + bonus + remote days + title — expand the pie. Use objective criteria (industry data).
- Bargain: if their cap is below your reservation, ask "How am I supposed to accept below market for this scope?" If no movement, walk to BATNA.
- Close: agreed package exceeds BATNA on weighted total.

**Personal/Family — Spouse financial disagreement (high-stakes relationship):**
- Prepare: separate spouse from problem. Interests: financial security, autonomy, family future, recognition. Their interests may overlap on family future even if position differs.
- Open: label their emotion — "It sounds like you're worried we won't have enough if we make this purchase." Seek "that's right."
- Style: collaborative (long relationship, high stakes). Avoid competing — relationship over single issue.
- Explore: invent options (delay purchase, scale purchase, alternative uses of saving). Add issues: vacation fund, retirement anxiety.
- Bargain: objective criteria (household budget rule, savings rate benchmark).
- Close: agreement protecting both financial and relationship interests.

**Diplomatic — Multi-party climate treaty:**
- Prepare: scope all parties. Multi-issue (emissions cuts, finance transfers, verification). Miniaturize: involve all in education, then subgroup substantive. Payoff matrix: country × issue × position × interest × coalition BATNA.
- Style: collaborative across coalitions, competing within concession distribution.
- Open: confidence-building measures, neutral facilitator. Cross-cutting groups pre-negotiation.
- Explore: One-Text procedure — single draft, iterative edits across delegations. Find trades (finance to developing for emissions cuts).
- Critical: maintain neutrality perception (don't visibly favor any bloc).
- Close: ratification process, implementation schedule, monitoring.

**Community — Apartment neighbor noise dispute (escalation recovery):**
- Prepare: bilateral, ongoing relationship. Interests: your peace, their lifestyle. Their BATNA: escalate to landlord/police.
- Escalation check: neighbor parked you as "enemy" → STOP substantive talk; cross-channel communication (mail or shared neighbor) before direct.
- Open: label ("It seems like the noise floor here isn't working for either of us.") → empathy.
- Explore: trade quiet hours for you / party-hours-notice for them. One-text: written agreement.
- Close: written, post at both doors, revisit in 2 weeks.

**Legal — Civil settlement (distributive + asymmetric info):**
- Prepare: BATNA = expected trial outcome × probability. Reservation point = expected value minus legal cost + risk premium.
- Distributive mode primarily: single issue (money), one-shot, low trust.
- Style: competing default, compromising if close to trial date.
- Objective criteria: comparable verdicts, similar fact patterns.
- Walk: if offer < reservation point, file for trial.

## Scope and Limitations

**Works on:** Two-or-more-party situations with partially opposed interests and the possibility of a deal better than the alternatives. Business deals, salary negotiation, diplomatic accords, family disputes, community discussions, legal settlements, internal team conflicts, multi-stakeholder decisions.

**Does NOT work on:**
- **Single-party decisions** with no counterpart → use `wrap`
- **Adversarial testing of own plan** → use `red-team`
- **Hypothesis evaluation** → use `ach`
- **Stakeholder mapping without engagement** → use `systems-thinking` or `link-analysis`
- **Technical contradiction resolution** → use `triz`

**Limits within scope:**
1. **No ZOPA after good-faith exploration** → walk to BATNA. Document the no-deal decision.
2. **Counterparty refuses good faith / uses dirty tricks** → negotiation jujitsu (don't attack back, deflect to merits). If sustained bad faith, walk to BATNA. Don't disclose integrative information when counterparty is purely distributive.
3. **Glasl Tier 3 escalation** → defer to mediation or third-party facilitation. This skill's scope ends at Tier 2.
4. **Severe power asymmetry with weak BATNA** → develop BATNA first. If you cannot strengthen BATNA, negotiation likely fails; pursuing BATNA IS the next-best move.
5. **Identity / principled values conflicts** → harder; pure values disputes resist trade. Accommodate on values, collaborate on procedural envelope. May require external mediation first.
6. **Cultural mismatch** → Fisher-Ury is Western-centric. Where direct interest articulation is culturally unacceptable, use mediators, indirect channels, longer relationship-building phases.
7. **Information so asymmetric you cannot estimate counterpart reservation** → ZOPA estimation fails. Defer to objective criteria + iterative calibrated questions to surface actual reservation over rounds.
8. **Reactive emotions overwhelm substance** → pause. Apply EI layer fully. If you cannot self-regulate, request recess. Negotiating while emotionally flooded equals giving your BATNA away.
